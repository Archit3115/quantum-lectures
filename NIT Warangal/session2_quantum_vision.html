First principles · the raw material

The computer never sees a cat.

It sees a spreadsheet of brightness values. Computer vision is the art of turning that grid back into meaning.

an image is a grid of numbers
  • Greyscale = a matrix of intensities (0 black … 255 white)
  • Colour = three such matrices (R, G, B)
  • A 28×28 digit = 784 numbers. A 1-MP colour photo = 3 million.
why raw pixels are terrible
  • High-dimensional — hundreds to millions of features
  • No built-in locality — neighbouring pixels are just two flat entries
  • Not translation-invariant — shift the cat 2px and every number changes

Vision is about learning good representations: local, hierarchical, invariant.

The classical tools · the atom

Convolution: slide a small window, combine.

the operation

Slide a small kernel (e.g. 3×3) over the image; at each stop, compute a weighted sum. Different kernels detect different things — a Sobel kernel finds edges, a blur kernel averages.

(I∗K)(i,j) = Σm Σn I(i+m, j+n)·K(m,n)

This "slide-a-window-and-combine" operation is the atom of all of vision.

Remember it — the quantum method replaces exactly this atom.

The classical tools · the revolution

The CNN — features that learn themselves.

AlexNet (2012) halved ImageNet error and started the deep-learning era. The key idea: don't hand-design the kernels — learn them from data.

Conv layer

Many learnable kernels slide over the image → feature maps. Edges → textures → objects.

ReLU

Keep positive responses, zero the rest → models non-linear patterns.

Pooling

Max/average over a window → shrinks the map, buys translation invariance.

FC + softmax

Flatten final features → classify. Trained end-to-end by backprop.

Three structural priors make CNNs work: local connectivity (a pixel relates to its neighbours) · weight sharing (one edge-detector, useful everywhere → far fewer parameters) · hierarchy (simple features compose into complex ones). These recur in the quantum story.

The classical tools · where it strains

State-of-the-art vision is enormous.

Beyond CNNs

Vision Transformers (2020) treat an image as patches and use attention — strong at scale, hungrier still.

The bill

Frontier models cost millions of dollars and megawatt-hours to train, on oceans of labelled data.

The opening

Quantum-enhanced methods aim here: richer features from fewer parameters, or feature spaces classical nets can't cheaply reach.

The quantum turn · the real question

How do you feed an image to a quantum computer?

Data encoding is 80% of quantum ML for vision. Three schemes — a trade-off between qubits and cost.

Basis

Classical bitstring → computational basis state.

N bits → N qubits

Wasteful; rarely used.

Angle ★

Each feature → a rotation angle Ry(xi) on a qubit.

N features → N qubits

Shallow, NISQ-friendly. How real images reach real hardware today.

Amplitude

Features → amplitudes of the state vector.

N features → ⌈log₂N⌉ qubits

Exponential compression — but expensive to prepare (QRAM).

Angle encoding turns one small patch of pixels into a few rotation angles — that's what makes quanvolution run today. Amplitude encoding is the exponential dream, waiting on hardware.

The quantum turn · the hero idea

Quanvolution — replace the atom with a circuit.

the recipe (Henderson et al., 2019)
  • Slide a 2×2 window — exactly like classical convolution
  • Angle-encode the 4 pixels on 4 qubits
  • Apply a random/trainable circuit — entangling gates mix pixels in a 16-D Hilbert space
  • Measure each qubit → new feature-map channels
  • Stack them, feed a classical CNN head

A hybrid: quantum front-end, classical back-end. The pattern for all near-term quantum vision.

the actual circuit (PennyLane)
Live demo · quantum feature maps

One 2×2 circuit → four views of every digit.

Each measured channel is a different learned-by-physics view of the digit — the direct analogue of a classical CNN's feature maps, except no training produced them; the entangling circuit did. An untrained 4-qubit circuit preserved ~86% of the classification signal.

The quantum turn · the native cousin

The QCNN — a CNN built from qubits.

Quantum convolution

The same two-qubit unitary applied across neighbouring qubits — weight sharing, natively.

Quantum pooling

Measure some qubits, condition rotations on the outcome → dimensionality reduction.

The payoff

Only O(log n) parameters, trainable, and relatively resistant to barren plateaus.

(Cong, Choi & Lukin, 2019.) Native use case: classifying quantum states / phases of matter — where the "image" is quantum data and there is no classical shortcut. For classical images it still needs encoding first.

The honest picture

No advantage on natural images yet — and why it still matters.

the caveats — say them out loud
  • On MNIST/CIFAR, tuned classical CNNs still win. Quanvolution's gains are modest and setting-dependent.
  • Encoding cost can eat the speedup — a megapixel image hits the same QRAM wall.
  • NISQ + shot noise — expectation values need many measurements; demos run in simulation or on tiny patches.
where the promise is genuine
  • Quantum-native data — QCNN classifies phases with no classical encoding
  • structured / scientific imaging where a quantum kernel is hard to simulate
  • feature richness from O(log n) parameters — for data-scarce scientific domains
